'King Arthur: Legend of the Sword' is two movies in one, and one is way better than the other

143
Shares
Share
Tweet
Share
What's This?
By Angie Han2017-05-11 20:08:38 UTC

The pitch for King Arthur: Legend of the Sword is "Guy Ritchie does medieval fantasy." Or so it would seem based on the marketing, which tries very hard to convince you that it is cool and different and Not Your Father's King Arthur Movie.

The trailers are set to pop songs, and emphasize Arthur's upbringing in the mean streets of Londinium. Arthur himself has the body of a superhero (and the camera spends plenty of time leering at it, lest you fail to notice). The action scenes do that speed-ramping thing.

But King Arthur feels less like "Guy Ritchie does medieval fantasy" than it does "Guy Ritchie coexists uneasily alongside medieval fantasy." There are two different movies inside King Arthur one an epic big-budget blockbuster, the other a slick heist comedy and one of them works way better than the other.

King Arthur, the generic fantasy blockbuster

For the first fifteen minutes or so, King Arthur is a total drag. The film opens with some text about the ancient grudge between man and mage or some such, and then leads into a big, gray-brown battle that pits one faceless CG army against another. If you'd told me the scene had been lifted straight out of Snow White and the Huntsman or Jack the Giant Slayer or The Hobbit or any of the other countless Lord of the Rings ripoffs we've seen in the past 15 years, I probably would've believed you.

Anyway, all of this is prelude to the real plot, which seems like a standard-issue Chosen One narrative. The king and queen send Baby Arthur up the river in a boat (like Moses, or that baby orc from Warcraft), and he eventually comes into the care of a bunch of common prostitutes. They have no idea who he really is, and so he doesn't either.

It's not until Excalibur emerges that Arthur realizes his true heritage, and even then it takes a lot of moody soul-searching and zippy training montages to get him to embrace his destiny and become the king he was always meant to be. Snore.

It doesn't help that most of the characters involved are deadly dull. Star Charlie Hunnam is clearly aiming for "lovable asshole," but with no one interesting to play off of, he just comes across as a garden-variety asshole. (Even before he learns he's royalty, he's the kind of dude who sees a giant line and decides it's okay to just shove his way to the front and pick fights with anyone who complains about it.)

Even the villain, Vortigern, is disappointingly colorless. Jude Law wears extravagant fur coats and snotty facial expressions like nobody's business, but never quite fulfills the scenery-chewing potential of such a clearly, obviously evil character. The closest he gets is in a scene involving a severed ear, which inspires one of the biggest laughs of the movie.

Occasionally, King Arthur loosens up a bit and starts to veer into "D&D manual on steroids" mode. There's a race of Ursula-style octopus mermaids who deal in dark magic, and Rodents of Unusual Size that probably can't be called Rodents of Unusual Size for copyright reasons. But King Arthur doesn't dwell on those details long enough to really make anything of them.

King Arthur, the slick heist comedy

Then there's the other movie inside King Arthur, which is basically a Guy Ritchie crime comedy set in the Middle Ages. This seems like the movie King Arthur should've been all along.

After that boring opening battle, King Arthur launches into a frenetic montage that gets us up to speed on Arthur's childhood and adolescence as a street urchin in Londinium. As Daniel Pemberton's energetic score sets the mood, we watch Arthur grow from a confused little kid to a very ripped Charlie Hunnam.

It's playful and it's surprising and it's deeply silly. (Apparently, the adult Arthur spends a lot of his time not wearing a shirt and punching the air.) In other words, it's exactly what I wanted out of a Guy Ritchie King Arthur movie.

It's also completely jarring, because it feels like an entirely different film from the one that came before it. As it turns out, that awkward transition is King Arthur in a nutshell. The movie spends the rest of its running time jumping between those two modes, rather than blending them into any coherent whole.

And one of those modes is far, far better than the other. The "Guy Ritchie heist comedy" parts of King Arthur aren't wholly successful there are way too many montages, and the characters aren't colorful enough to really sell the slick vibe but there's a liveliness that's sorely lacking in the "epic drama" parts. It feels like Richie's having fun bringing his breezy, modern style to ancient England.

It doesn't feel like anyone's having fun when King Arthur returns to the serious bits. At the very least, I know I'm not. Had King Arthur really committed to its "crime comedy" side, it might've been a refreshing standout in the summer blockbuster landscape. As it is, it's just another generic blockbuster that'll be forgotten by the time the next generic blockbuster rolls into theaters. Too bad.
